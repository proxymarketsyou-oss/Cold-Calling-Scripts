Primary Service

Meta Advertisements

NRS open → qual flip → Four W → bridge → present → mirror close → pullback → game plan + close

1
NRS Open · 0–5 seconds
Pattern interrupt — no resistance, instant hook
"Hey [Name] — quick question, are you the one who handles marketing for [Business]?"

✅ When confirmed (they handle it + run ads):

"Perfect. I'll be straight with you — I run Meta ad campaigns for [type of business], and I came across your page. I noticed something that's actively costing you leads every single day. Figured a 2-minute call was better than you finding out the hard way. Does that sound fair?"

🚫 When they don't run Meta ads:

"That actually makes this call more interesting — most businesses in [industry] are getting undercut by competitors who are running ads, and they don't even know it. You're getting traffic from somewhere right now — I just want to show you what's being left on the table. Is that a fair two minutes?"
Coaching tipDon't treat "we don't run ads" as a dead end — it's actually the best possible signal. They're the untapped opportunity. Reframe it as them having an advantage if they move first, not as a problem. Keep the energy warm and curious, not salesy.

↪ When it's not their department (wrong person):

"No worries at all — who's the best person to speak to about that? I just want to make sure I'm not wasting anyone's time, including yours."
PsychologyDon't pitch to the wrong person — it kills the lead. Stay warm, make it feel effortless for them to redirect you. "I don't want to waste your time" lowers their guard and makes them far more likely to give you the real name rather than just brushing you off.
NRS principleYou're not asking for their time — you're framing yourself as delivering value they'd be crazy to refuse. No pitch. No resistance. Daniel G: "Open conversations that instantly lower resistance."
2
Qualification Flip · Gatekeeper Open
Make them want to qualify — shift from "do I want this" to "am I good enough"
Say this
"Before I go any further — I want to be upfront. The way I work isn't the right fit for every business, and I'd hate to waste your time if the timing isn't right. I've found my approach works really well in certain situations, but definitely not all. Does that make sense?"
PsychologyYou didn't say no. You didn't fake scarcity. You implied there's a process they might not pass. Their brain instantly shifts — now they're trying to qualify themselves to you, not the other way around.
3
NRS Four W Questions · Identity-Based Qualifying
What, Where, Why, What if — get them to say the pain out loud
NRS — Four W Framework

Ask in order. One at a time. Silence is your friend — let them talk.

W1 — What
"What does your marketing actually look like right now — are you running any Meta ads, or is it mostly organic posts?"
W2 — Where
"Where are your leads coming from right now? Like if I asked you what's driving the most business — what would you say?"
W3 — Why
"Why do you think the ads haven't been consistent — is it the budget, the creative, or just not knowing what's working?"
W4 — What if
"What if your ads were generating 10–15 qualified leads a week on autopilot — what would that change for the business?"
PsychologyW4 is the identity anchor. They've now visualized the outcome in their own words. You haven't pitched anything. They've sold themselves. Now transition directly into the presentation — don't loop back to selectivity. Say: "Okay — based on what you just told me, let me show you exactly how I'd fix this."
4
Transition into Present · W4 Bridge
Use their W4 answer to lead into the presentation — don't restart the selectivity pitch
Say this — bridge directly from W4
"Okay — so based on what you just told me, here's what I'd actually do for you specifically..."

Then go straight into Step 5 (Problem-Centric Present). Do NOT circle back to "I'm selective" or "I only work with certain businesses" — that was already set in Step 2. Repeating it here kills the momentum you just built. The Four W answers are your segue into the presentation, not a reason to re-qualify them.

PsychologyAfter W4 they've emotionally bought in. The worst thing you can do is pump the brakes with another gatekeeping line. Bridge straight to the presentation using their own words from W4 — mirror their language back to show you were actually listening.
5
Problem-Centric Present · NRS Principle
Present around their problem — not your service
NRS — Problem-Centric
"Based on what you told me — you're getting some results but it's inconsistent, and you're not sure what's actually working. That's the exact gap I close. I build Meta campaigns that are data-driven from day one — we test creative, audiences, and offers until we find what converts, then we scale it. The businesses I work with go from guessing to having a predictable system within 30–60 days."
NRS principleDon't list features. Lead with their problem, show the bridge, land on the outcome. Daniel G: present around the buyer's pain, not your product.
6
Mirror Close · Sequence 1
You decide they qualify — flip the power dynamic completely
Never ask "want to move forward?"
"Honestly, from everything you just told me — I think this is a really strong fit. The real question isn't whether this works. It's just whether this feels like the right move for where you're trying to take the business."
PsychologyYou declared they're qualified — that removes the "am I good enough" pressure. The only remaining question is their own commitment to their identity. They're not answering you anymore, they're answering themselves.
7
Final Filter + Pullback · Sequence 2
Speed test — then give them the out so they chase you
Sequence 2A
"Perfect — just so I'm clear, you're ready to get this moving this week? Because like I mentioned, this only works for people who move fast once they decide."

Add immediately after:

"Of course, there's absolutely no pressure to decide today. You're completely free to take as long as you need."
If they hesitate — Sequence 2B (Pullback)
"No worries at all — you're completely free to take all the time you need. Honestly, I'd rather you be sure. Like I said, I'm selective about who I bring on — I'd genuinely rather wait for the right fit than rush someone who isn't ready."
PsychologyThe pullback triggers reactance — the psychological law that says people want what's being taken away. The more you release, the more they chase. Hesitation almost always collapses here.
8
NRS Objection Handling · 3-Layer Approach
Agree → reframe → close — never fight an objection
Objection: "I've tried ads before and they didn't work"
"That makes complete sense — and honestly, most ads people have run before were set up to fail. The targeting, the creative, the offer — one weak link and the whole thing bleeds money. What you tried before and what I do are very different. That's actually exactly why I'd want to work with you — because you already know what not to do."
Objection: "I don't have the budget right now"
"I hear you — and honestly, if budget's tight that's actually more reason to make sure every dollar you do spend is working. If I told you every $1,000 you put in came back as $3,500, would the budget still be the issue?"
Objection: "Let me think about it / talk to my partner"
"Absolutely — you're completely free to take your time. Just one question before you go — is there something specific you'd need to get clear on? Because if there's a real concern, I'd rather address it now so you're not sitting on a half-picture."
NRS principleDaniel G's 3-layer: agree with the objection → it's hard to fight someone who agrees with you → reframe toward the real concern → soft close. Never argue. Sell like a lamb.
9
Game Plan & Close · 30-Day Plan
Neutralise every objection before it arrives — money, time, and skill — then close
Game Plan Open
"Based on what you told me, I think there's a clear path to fixing this. I can put together a simple 30-day game plan showing what we'd change, how we'd structure the campaigns, and how we'd get you toward more leads coming in consistently. We can walk through it on a quick 20-minute call this week. What works better for you — Thursday or Friday?"

If they ask what the plan looks like before committing to the call, give them a quick preview:

Week 1–2 — Foundation
"We set up the campaign infrastructure properly — pixel, audiences, creative brief, offer positioning. Everything that's been missing or broken gets fixed before a single dollar goes live."
Week 3–4 — First Results
"We go live with a structured campaign, monitor what's converting, and make real-time adjustments. By day 30 you're not guessing anymore — you can see exactly what's working and what each lead is costing you."
Coaching noteDon't over-explain the plan on this call — the goal is to get them to the 20-minute walkthrough. Give just enough for them to see it's structured and real, then stop and let the close land.
or — if they're already sold on the call
Skip the game plan — close on the proposal directly
"Honestly — I've got everything I need from this call. If you're ready, I can put together the proposal today and have it with you by tomorrow morning. Does that work?"
When to use thisIf they've already said yes, or the energy is clearly there — don't slow it down with the game plan call. Go straight to the proposal. The game plan is for people who need one more reason. People who are ready just need a next step.

Web Design

NRS open → qual flip → Four W → bridge → present → mirror close → pullback → hard close

1
NRS Open · 0–5 seconds
Lead with loss — make the problem undeniable
Say this
"Hey [Name] — I just pulled up your website and I genuinely think you're losing customers before they ever get a chance to call you. It's a fixable problem and I wanted to reach out personally. Are you the right person to talk to about that?"
NRS principle"Losing customers" is visceral and specific. You've already done research on them — instant credibility. You're not pitching, you're flagging a problem they didn't know was costing them money.
2
Qualification Flip · Gatekeeper Open
Set the frame — make them prove they belong
Say this
"Before I go into what I found — I want to be honest with you. What I do isn't right for every business, and I'd hate for either of us to waste time. I work with businesses that are established and know their product is solid, but their online presence isn't doing them justice. I'm very selective. If the timing's off, I'd rather know now."
PsychologyYou've reframed the conversation. They now have to prove they're the kind of business worth working on — not the other way around.
3
NRS Four W Questions
Get them to quantify the gap themselves
NRS — Four W Framework
W1 — What
"What does your website actually do for you right now — are you getting enquiries from it, or is it more just kind of there?"
W2 — Where
"Where is most of your new business coming from — referrals, word of mouth, online?"
W3 — Why
"Why do you think the site isn't converting — is it the design, the copy, or people just aren't finding it?"
W4 — What if
"What if your website was your best salesperson — working 24/7, converting visitors into calls and bookings automatically — what would that be worth to the business monthly?"
PsychologyW4 forces them to put a dollar figure on the outcome in their own head. You haven't quoted a price yet — but they've already decided what the result is worth to them. Now bridge directly into your presentation using their answer — say: "Okay — so based on what you just told me, here's exactly what I'd build for you." Don't loop back to the selectivity framing you already set in Step 2.
4
Transition into Present · W4 Bridge
Their W4 answer is your segue — use it, don't restart the selectivity pitch
Bridge from W4 — go straight to the present
"Okay — so you said [mirror their W4 answer]. That's exactly what I fix. Let me tell you specifically what I'd do for your site..."

Skip the "two types of clients" framing here — you already set that expectation in Step 2. Going back to it kills the momentum from the Four W's. Move directly into Step 5.

PsychologyAfter W4 they've already priced the outcome in their head. Jumping back into gatekeeping language now creates cognitive friction — it feels like a pitch, not a conversation. Bridge with their words, go into the present.

NRS Objection Handling
Agree → reframe → soft close
Objection: "We already have a website"
"Of course — most businesses do. The question isn't whether you have one, it's whether it's actually working for you. You told me yourself enquiries aren't coming from it. Having a site and having a site that converts are two completely different things."
Objection: "It's not in the budget right now"
"That's fair — and that's exactly why I ask the 'what would it be worth' question. If a new site brought in even 2 new clients a month that you weren't getting before, how long before it pays for itself? Budget decisions are really ROI decisions."
Objection: "I need to think about it"
"Absolutely — take your time. Before you go though — is there a specific concern? I'd rather give you the full picture now than have you thinking about something I could clear up in 30 seconds."

1
NRS Open · 0–5 seconds
Specificity kills the guard — name exactly what you saw
Say this
"Hey [Name] — I was just on [Business]'s Instagram and I noticed the last post was [X weeks/months] ago. I work with businesses on their social presence and I can tell you — that silence is actively costing you customers right now. I wanted to reach out personally. Are you the right person to talk to?"
NRS principleSpecificity proves you actually looked — instant credibility. "Actively costing you" activates loss aversion, the strongest psychological motivator. You're not selling — you're warning.
2
Qualification Flip · Gatekeeper Open

Say this
"Before I get into what I found — I want to be straight. What I do isn't for every business and I'd hate to waste your time. Psychology"Take their brand seriously" is identity language. Every business owner believes they take their brand seriously — and now they've committed to that frame before you've asked for anything.
