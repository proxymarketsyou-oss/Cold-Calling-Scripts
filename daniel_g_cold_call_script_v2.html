● Meta Ads

Meta Advertisements Script

Full Daniel G sequence — every branch covered. Read every step before your first call.

1
Step 1 — Open · First 10 seconds
Pattern interrupt — position yourself, earn the conversation
Always open with this — word for word
"Hey [Name] — did I catch you at a bad time?"
Daniel G — Why this line worksEvery other salesperson opens with a pitch. You open with respect for their time. When they say "no, go ahead" they've given you permission to speak — and permission changes the dynamic completely. You're no longer an intruder. You're a guest.

✅ When they say "no, go ahead" / "what's this about":

"I run Meta ad campaigns specifically for [type of business — e.g. local gyms, roofing companies, dental clinics] and I came across [Business Name]. I noticed you're either not running ads right now or your current setup isn't structured to convert — and for a business like yours, that's leads walking past you every single day. I've got one quick question for you. Is that a fair 60 seconds?"
Coaching tip — "I noticed""I noticed something" is a curiosity gap. They want to know what you saw. It also proves you actually looked at their business — instant credibility over every other cold caller who clearly didn't. Don't explain the full solution yet. You're just earning the right to ask questions.

🚫 If they say "yes, bad time" or "I'm busy":

"Totally fine — when would be a better time to give you a call back? I'll be quick, I promise it'll be worth 60 seconds."
Coaching tipDon't push when they say bad time. Lock in a callback immediately — don't just say "I'll try again later." Get a specific day. "Is tomorrow morning better, or afternoon?" The moment you have a time, you've converted the call into a warm follow-up.

🚫 If they don't run any ads at all — don't treat it as a dead end:

"That actually makes this call more interesting — because most businesses in [industry] are getting undercut by competitors who are running ads right now, and they don't even know it. Your competitors are showing up in front of people who are searching for exactly what you do — and you're not there. You've got a first-mover window here. Do you have 90 seconds?"
Mindset shift"We don't run ads" is the best possible opening. It means zero competition from themselves — they haven't wasted money on bad ads and they're not comparing you to a previous provider. Reframe their blank slate as an advantage, not a problem.

↪ If it's the wrong person (not the decision-maker):

"No worries at all — who's the right person to speak to about marketing decisions? I just want to make sure I'm not wasting anyone's time, including yours."
Psychology"I don't want to waste your time" lowers the guard and makes them far more likely to give you the real name instead of brushing you off. Never pitch to the gatekeeper — you'll get a soft no passed on as a hard one.

↪ If they run ads but with someone else already:

"That's great — are you happy with what you're getting from them right now? I ask because a lot of businesses I speak to are running ads and spending money but can't actually tell what's working. Is that the case or are yours converting well?"
Coaching tipDon't retreat the second you hear "we have an agency." Ask one honest question. If they pause before saying "yeah it's great" — that hesitation is your opening. If they genuinely are happy, respect it and move on. Don't waste either person's time.
2
Step 2 — Qualify · Four W Questions
What, Where, Why, What if — make them say the problem out loud
Daniel G · Four W Framework

Ask one question at a time. Wait for the full answer. Do not rush. Do not move to W2 while they're still answering W1. Silence is your friend — let it breathe.

W1 — What
"What does your current marketing actually look like — are you running any paid ads at all right now, or is it mostly organic?"
W2 — Where
"Where are your leads coming from right now — if I asked you what's actually driving the most new business, what would you say?"
W3 — Why
"Why do you think paid ads haven't been a consistent part of the plan so far — is it the budget, not knowing if they'd work, or just not having had the time to set it up properly?"
W4 — What if
"What if you had a proper system bringing in 10 to 20 qualified leads a month from Meta — not random clicks, but people who are actually looking for what you do — what would that change for the business?"
W4 — The close setup. This is the most important question you'll ask.Let them answer in full. Write down their exact words. Whatever they say — "we'd be able to hire someone", "we could finally scale", "I'd stop stressing about where the next client is coming from" — that is your close. You will mirror it back in Step 4 word for word. Do not move on until they've answered W4.

⚡ What if they give very short answers and don't open up?

"Got it — and just so I understand it better, can you give me a bit more on that? Like on a typical month, roughly how many new enquiries are you getting?"
Coaching tipShort answers usually mean they're cautious, not uninterested. Ask a follow-up that requires a specific number or example. Numbers make people think concretely — and once they're thinking concretely, they open up.

⚡ Bridge straight from W4 into Step 3 — do not go back to a qualifying frame:

"Okay — so based on what you just told me, here's exactly what I'd do for [Business Name] specifically..."
PsychologyAfter W4, they've emotionally bought in. Pump the brakes with another "let me tell you about my process" line and you'll kill the momentum. Use their exact W4 words to open your present. Mirror, don't introduce.
3
Step 3 — Present · Problem-First
Name their problem, bridge to the outcome — under 60 seconds, no feature lists

Lead with their problem, not your service. Problem → bridge → outcome. That's the entire structure.

"Based on what you told me — you're relying on referrals and word of mouth, which works until it doesn't. The problem is you have no control over it. Some months are great, some months are dry, and there's nothing you can do to change it. That's the exact gap I close. I build Meta campaigns specifically for [type of business] — right audience, right creative, right offer — so you go from hoping leads show up to having a system that brings them in consistently. The businesses I work with typically see their first real results within 30 to 60 days and within 90 days they know their exact cost per lead. It stops being a gamble."
Daniel G — present principleNo feature dumps. No "I offer A/B testing, pixel setup, retargeting…" One problem. One bridge. One outcome. The moment you list features you sound like every other marketer. The moment you name their specific situation back to them, you sound like the only person who actually understands it.

🚫 If they've tried ads before and they didn't work — adjust the present:

"Based on what you described — you've tried ads before and they bled money. That tells me one of three things went wrong: the targeting was too broad, the creative wasn't stopping the scroll, or the offer wasn't compelling enough for cold traffic. Any one of those will sink a campaign. What I do differently is build a proper structure from day one — test small, find what converts, then scale only what's proven. The businesses I work with who've been burned before are usually the most motivated to do it right."

🚫 If they don't have a landing page / website to send traffic to — don't ignore it:

"Before I go further — one thing that's going to affect the results is where we send the traffic. Running ads without a proper landing page is like running a TV commercial with no phone number. Do you have a website right now, or are people going to a Facebook page?"
Pivot triggerIf they say they don't have a website or it's very basic — this is your signal to consider pivoting to web design first. See the Pivot Scripts tab for the Meta → Web Design full script.
4
Step 4 — Close · Daniel G Method
"Would you be against…" — stack the value, then the pullback
Primary close — use this first
"Would you be against jumping on a 20-minute call this week where I walk you through exactly what I'd build for [Business Name] — the targeting, the offer structure, what the first 30 days looks like — so you can see whether it makes sense before committing to anything?"
Why "would you be against" worksIt's a double negative. There's no bad answer for you. If they say yes — they agree. If they say no — they're open. It also reframes the call as you giving them information, not selling. You're removing pressure at the exact moment most salespeople add it.
if they say yes to the call
"Perfect — what's better for you, Thursday or Friday? I'll keep it to 20 minutes, I'll have some specifics pulled together for your business beforehand."
Coaching tip — the assumptive calendar closeDon't ask "does that sound good?" — ask which day. Giving two options removes the binary yes/no entirely. They're now choosing between Thursday and Friday, not between yes and no.
if they're already warm — close direct on the proposal
"Based on everything you've told me — the reliance on referrals, the inconsistency, and what you said a proper pipeline would mean for you — I've got a clear picture of what I'd do. Would you be against me putting a proposal together today so you can have it in your inbox by tomorrow morning — scope, timeline, investment — and we go from there?"
if they hesitate — the pullback
"Completely fine — no pressure at all. Genuinely. You're free to take as much time as you need. I only work with a handful of businesses at a time and I'd rather the fit be right than rush someone into something they're not fully sure about. If the timing isn't right, would it make more sense to circle back in a few weeks?"
Psychology — the pullbackThe moment you release pressure, the hesitation often collapses. "I'd rather wait for the right fit" signals you don't need them — which paradoxically makes them want to move. Nine times out of ten they re-engage rather than defer. If they defer — get a specific date, add it to your calendar, and follow up exactly then.
if they ask about price before the call
"I don't quote on a cold call — because until I understand your specific situation properly, any number I give you would be made up. What I can tell you is I work with businesses of all sizes and I structure it around what makes sense for the return. That's what the 20-minute call is for. Fair?"
GP
Game Plan · 30-Day Breakdown — use if they need more before committing to a call
Give just enough structure to make the call feel worth it — then stop
"Let me give you a quick picture of what the first 30 days actually looks like. Week one and two — we set the foundation. That means the pixel, proper audience targeting, offer positioning, and creative brief. Everything that's normally skipped or broken gets sorted before a single pound or dollar goes live. Week three and four — we go live with a controlled test. We monitor what's converting, make real-time adjustments, and by day 30 you know exactly what your cost per lead is. No more guessing. That's the walkthrough call in 20 minutes — would Thursday or Friday work better for you?"
Critical ruleDo NOT over-explain the plan on this call. The goal is to get them to the 20-minute walkthrough — not to do the entire strategy session right now. Give enough that they can see it's real and structured. Then stop and let the close land. If you keep talking, you talk yourself out of the booking.

● Web Design

Web Design Script

Specific-problem open → conversion-focused Four W → present → close

1
Step 1 — Open · Lead with what you saw
You looked at their site before calling — make that obvious
Always start with this
"Hey [Name] — did I catch you at a bad time?"

Once they say go ahead:

"I just came across [Business Name]'s website and I genuinely think it's losing you customers before they ever get a chance to call you. I build conversion-focused websites for [type of business] and I could see three specific things straight away that are costing you enquiries. Are you the right person to speak to about that?"
Daniel G — why specificity wins the open"I could see three specific things" is a curiosity gap. They want to know what the three things are. They don't know whether to be defensive or curious — and that gap buys you 60 seconds. You've also proven you actually looked, which puts you above every other cold caller automatically.

⚡ If they immediately ask "what three things?":

"I'll walk you through all of them — I just want to ask you a couple of quick questions first so I understand what you're actually trying to get the site to do for you. That way I'm not just pointing out problems without knowing what matters most to your business. Is that fair?"
Coaching tipDon't give the three things away before you qualify. If you do, you become a free consultant. Gate the detail behind the Four W — it also positions your diagnosis as more valuable because they had to wait for it.

🚫 If they say they're happy with the site / just had it rebuilt:

"That's great — when you say you're happy with it, is that from a design standpoint or is it actually bringing in enquiries? Because those are two different things. A site can look great and still not convert. Which way is it for you right now?"
Coaching tipThis question forces them to separate aesthetics from performance. Almost every business owner is proud of how their site looks but unsure whether it's working. That uncertainty is your opening.

🚫 If they don't have a website at all:

"That's actually the most important thing to fix first — before you spend a penny on ads, social, or anything else. Right now every time someone Googles you or hears about you from someone else, they have nowhere to go to get confident enough to call. You're losing business every single week without knowing it. Are you the right person to make that decision?"
2
Step 2 — Qualify · Four W Questions
Traffic problem or conversion problem — you need to know which one before you present
Daniel G · Four W Framework
W1 — What
"What is the site actually doing for you right now — are you getting enquiries from it, or is it more just sitting there as a digital business card?"
W2 — Where
"Where is most of your new business coming from — is anyone actually finding you online or is it mostly referrals and word of mouth?"
W3 — Why
"Why do you think the site isn't converting — is it the design, the copy, people aren't finding it in the first place, or a bit of all three?"
W4 — What if
"What if your website was your best salesperson — working 24 hours a day, turning visitors into calls and bookings automatically — what would that be worth to the business every month?"
The W4 ROI anchor — criticalThey just put a number in their head before you've quoted anything. When they later object to price, they're arguing against their own math — not yours. That is a completely different and winnable conversation. Write down exactly what they say for W4.

⚡ Key diagnostic — listen to W1 and W2 together:

If W1 = "no enquiries" and W2 = "we get traffic from Instagram / Google" → conversion problem — the site is the leak. Present around fixing the conversion layer.

If W1 = "no enquiries" and W2 = "mostly word of mouth, no online traffic" → traffic + conversion problem — fix the site first, then suggest ads. (See Pivot Scripts for the Web → Meta Ads pivot.)

3
Step 3 — Present · Name the right problem
Fix the leak before you fix the tap — two versions based on their answers
Bridge from W4 — always open with this
"So you said [mirror their W4 answer]. That's the gap I fix. Here's specifically what I'd do..."
Version A — they have traffic but no conversions
"You've already solved the hardest part — people are finding you. The site is just losing them the moment they land. What I build is designed around one thing: getting visitors to take action. Clear messaging, one primary call to action, trust signals that remove hesitation, fast load time. The businesses I've worked with have gone from 'site does nothing' to it being their number one lead source within 60 to 90 days. The site stops being a cost you maintain and becomes a revenue channel."
Version B — they have no traffic either (double problem)
"So there are two things happening — the site isn't converting, and you don't have consistent traffic. The order matters here. There's no point putting money into driving traffic to a site that won't convert it. You'd be filling a bucket with a hole in it. Fix the conversion layer first — then the traffic you drive actually turns into leads. I sort the foundation so any traffic you bring in, whether organic or paid, actually does something."
Coaching tip — the "foundation first" frameTelling someone to fix the site before spending on ads positions you as an advisor, not a salesperson. You're protecting their money. That trust is worth more than the sale of ads today — and it usually leads to a bigger retainer later.
4
Step 4 — Close · Daniel G Method
"Would you be against a mockup call" — site as investment, not cost
Primary close
"Would you be against jumping on a 20-minute call this week where I pull together a rough direction for your site and walk you through exactly what I'd change — so you can actually see what I'd build before making any decision?"
Why this worksYou're doing work before they commit. That give-before-you-get removes the risk in their mind entirely. A mockup call also lets you walk them through the ROI conversation with their own W4 numbers in front of you both — which makes the close on the proposal call almost effortless.
if they're already warm — close on the proposal
"Based on what you've told me — people are finding you and the site isn't converting any of them. That's a clean fix. Would you be against letting me put a proposal together this week — scope, timeline, investment — so you can look it over and we get the site working for you before the end of the month?"
if they hesitate — the pullback
"Completely understand — no pressure. The only thing worth saying is the site is losing leads every single day it stays the way it is. But genuinely — if the timing isn't right, I'd rather wait and do it properly. When would be a better time to pick this back up?"
if they ask about price
"I don't give numbers before I understand the full scope — because any figure I give you now would either undersell what you need or spook you with something that's not relevant. What I'd say is: you told me a site that converted would be worth [their W4 answer] a month. The investment is always a fraction of that. That's what the mockup call is for — I'll show you exactly what I'd do and the investment attached to it. Fair?"

● Social Media

Social Media Management Script

Page-specific open → W3 finds the real block → leads-not-likes present → close

1
Step 1 — Open · Reference their actual page
Specificity kills the guard — name exactly what you saw
Always open with this
"Hey [Name] — did I catch you at a bad time?"

Once they say go ahead:

"I was just on [Business Name]'s [Instagram / Facebook] page and I noticed the last post was [X days / weeks / months] ago. I manage social media for [type of business] and I'll be direct with you — that silence on the page is actively costing you customers right now. People check social before they decide to call. When it's quiet, they move on. Are you the right person to speak to about that?"
Daniel G — Specificity + loss aversion"I noticed the last post was [specific timeframe] ago" proves you looked. "Actively costing you customers" hits loss aversion — the strongest psychological motivator there is. You're not pitching a service. You're warning them about a problem they didn't know was costing them money today.

✅ If they post regularly but engagement is dead:

"I can see you're posting consistently — which is great. The issue is the content isn't converting the attention into enquiries. That's a strategy problem, not a consistency problem. You're doing the hard work and not getting the return from it."

🚫 If they barely have a presence / page is nearly empty:

"The page is pretty quiet right now — and in [industry], that's a real opportunity because most of your competitors aren't doing this properly either. The businesses that show up consistently are the ones people trust before they've even spoken to them. You don't need to be everywhere — you need to be consistent in one or two places. Do you have 90 seconds?"

🚫 If they say "we do our own social":

"That's great — is it bringing in actual enquiries, or is it more just for brand presence? I ask because there's a big difference between posting and having a strategy that actually moves people to reach out. Which way is it for you?"
Coaching tipMost business owners who "do their own social" are posting reactively with no strategy. They already know it's not working. This question gives them permission to admit it.
2
Step 2 — Qualify · Four W Questions
W3 is the key question — it tells you the real reason it's not happening
Daniel G · Four W Framework
W1 — What
"What does your social actually look like right now — are you posting consistently or is it more when you get a chance to?"
W2 — Where
"Where are your customers coming from right now — is social any part of that, or is it mostly referrals and word of mouth?"
W3 — Why
"Why has it been hard to stay consistent with it — is it the time it takes, not really knowing what to post, or it just keeps getting pushed down the list?"
W4 — What if
"What if your social was consistently generating enquiries every week — DMs turning into bookings, posts building trust before people even reach out — what would that change for the business?"
Psychology — W3 removes shameW3 gives them a permission slip. "I've been too busy" is not a failure — it's the exact problem you solve. When they say it, respond warmly: "That's exactly what I hear all the time — and it's exactly why I exist." Don't let them feel judged for it. That warmth builds trust fast.
W3 also tells you what to sell the promise aroundIf they say "time" → your hook is "completely off your plate." If they say "don't know what to post" → your hook is "full strategy included." If they say "it keeps getting deprioritised" → your hook is "I make it something that runs without you thinking about it." Match your present to the W3 answer.
3
Step 3 — Present · Social as Lead Engine
Leads not likes — position social as a pipeline, not a branding exercise
Bridge from W4 first
"So you said [mirror their exact W4 words]. That's what I build. Here's what that actually looks like for a business like yours..."
If W3 = "no time"
"From what you told me — you've got something real to show, the business is good, you just don't have the time to show it consistently. That's the only gap. I manage the social end to end — strategy, content creation, captions, scheduling — it's completely off your plate. You never have to think about it. The businesses I work with go from invisible to consistently showing up in front of the right people within the first 30 days. The DMs start coming in and they didn't have to do anything to make it happen."
If W3 = "don't know what to post"
"From what you told me — you know the business is good, you just don't know how to translate that into content that makes people want to call. That's a strategy problem and it's exactly what I solve. I build a full content strategy around your business — what to post, why, how often, and in what format — so that every piece of content is doing a job. Moving people from strangers to enquiries. You don't need to know what to post because that's my department."
If W3 = "it keeps getting deprioritised"
"From what you told me — you know it matters, it just keeps losing to the hundred other things running the business. That's the most common thing I hear. What I do is take it completely off the decision pile — it runs whether you're thinking about it or not. Within 30 days you've got a presence that's building trust and generating enquiries on its own. You get to focus on actually serving those clients."

Step 4 — Close · Daniel G Method
"Would you be against a sample strategy call" — done-for-you frame
Primary close
"Would you be against jumping on a quick call this week where I put together a sample content strategy for [Business Name] — actual post ideas, themes, the platform plan — so you can see exactly what I'd do before you decide anything?"
Why this worksYou're doing real work before asking for money. That removes risk entirely. The sample strategy call is your selling environment — you're in front of them, building value live, and by the end of the call the retainer is the logical next step, not a cold ask.
if they're already warm — close on a start date
"Honestly — from everything you've said, the inconsistency, the time it takes, what a proper pipeline from social would mean — this is a clean fix. Would you be against getting started this week so we can get the first month of content live and you can see the difference straight away?"
if they hesitate — the pullback
"Totally fine — no rush at all. The only thing worth saying is that every week without consistent content is another week your competitors are showing up in front of your customers and you're not. But if the timing isn't right, I'd genuinely rather wait and do it properly. When would be a better time to pick this back up?"
if they say they want to think about it
"Absolutely — take all the time you need. Before you go — is there one specific thing that's making you hesitate? Because most of the time when someone says they need to think about it there's one real concern underneath it. I'd much rather address that now than have you sitting on something I could clear up in 30 seconds."

● Objections

Objection Handling

Agree → reframe → re-close. Never argue. Never defend. Always redirect.

$
Price & Budget Objections
Redirect to ROI — they already did the math in W4
Objection: "It's not in the budget right now"
"That makes complete sense — and that's exactly why I ask what it would be worth if it worked. You said [their W4 answer]. If this brings in even two or three new clients a month you weren't getting before, how long before it pays for itself? I'm not asking you to spend money — I'm asking whether the return justifies the investment. And you already answered that."
Objection: "That's too expensive"
"Fair enough — what were you thinking? I ask because I'd rather find a way to make this work than walk away from something that could genuinely change your lead flow. The investment is always relative to what it returns. What does a new client actually mean to your business in revenue terms?"
Objection: "I can't justify that spend right now"
"I understand — and I'd never ask you to. The question isn't whether you can afford it right now. The question is whether staying where you are is more expensive than fixing it. Every month without a proper [ads system / converting site / social strategy] is a month of leads going to someone else. What's that worth to you over six months?"
The W4 anchor — why it matters hereIf you skipped W4 or they gave a vague answer, price objections are hard to answer. If you got a strong W4 answer, every price objection becomes an ROI conversation using their own numbers. This is why W4 is non-negotiable.

Trust & Credibility
Diagnose before you defend — asking "what went wrong" is more powerful than any case study
Objection: "I've tried this before and it didn't work"
"That makes complete sense — and honestly that's exactly the situation I hear most. What happened with it? I ask because most of the time when this kind of thing fails it comes down to one of three things: wrong targeting, weak creative, or no proper tracking so no one knew what was working. Which of those was it for you — do you know?"
Objection: "How do I know this will work for my business?"
"That's the right question. And I'd never ask you to take a leap of faith. Here's what I'd say: the strategy call I'm proposing costs you nothing. You get to see exactly what I'd do for your business specifically before committing to anything. If it doesn't make sense after that, you've lost 20 minutes. That's a pretty low-risk way to find out."
Objection: "We already have someone doing this"
"That's great — are you happy with what you're getting from them? I ask because most businesses I talk to either have someone and it's working well — in which case I'd say stick with it — or they have someone and they're not really sure it's moving the needle. Which camp are you in?"
Psychology — the credibility flipAsking "what went wrong" makes you the only person on the call not trying to convince them everything will be fine. That intellectual honesty is more persuasive than any testimonial. You're diagnosing before you're selling — which is what a trusted expert does, not a salesperson.
